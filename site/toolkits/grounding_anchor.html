Grounding Anchor

When the moment is too much —
land back in your body.

Grounding isn't a daily routine you keep up. It's what you reach for right now, when you're flooded, frozen, overstimulated, or spiralling. Start with breath, then pick the track that fits your brain.

5-4-3-2-1 · pull yourself into the room ▶
5See. Name five things you can see right now. Say them, out loud or in your head.
4Feel. Four things you can physically feel — the chair, your feet, fabric, air.
3Hear. Three sounds, near or far. Let them just be sounds.
2Smell. Two things you can smell — or two you like and can picture.
1Taste. One thing you can taste, or one you'd want to. You're here. It's now.
Which brain are you grounding today?
Pick a track above. Each one lists the moments that knock that brain off balance — tap the one that matches and you'll get grounding steps that actually fit.
A note on the OCD track: these steps are informed by exposure-and-response-prevention (ERP) — the front-line approach for OCD — but this is a self-help anchor, not therapy. The aim is to sit with uncertainty rather than chase certainty. If compulsions are running your days, working with an ERP-trained therapist is worth far more than any page.
Nothing here is medical advice, and you don't have to do all of it. Grounding is about turning the volume down enough to choose your next move — not about doing it "right." Take the one step that helps and leave the rest.
